\begin{abstract}
Context-aware and ubiquitous systems have the challenge of implicitly collect relevant information about the users, and use this information to understand and predict their behaviour. This allows the applications to adapt themselves to the user, thus avoiding to overflow the user with inquires and information. This article presents a model that allows applications to register users' actions and infer profile information from these actions, using semantic interoperability and thus allowing different applications of different domains to be able to share information and infer a unified profile.
\keywords{Profile Management \and Ubiquitous Computing \and Trails \and Profile Inference}

\section{Introduction}
\label{ch:introducao}
%\paragraph{Paragraph headings} Use paragraph headings as needed.

Nowadays, most users interact with a wide variety of computing systems. The current tendency is the increased interconnectedness between these systems, since the internet is becoming every day more present and available. Recent research predicts the full integration between these systems in a federation of computing systems. This federation is referred to as Smart Environment \citep{heckmann05}.

\citet{weiser91} discussed a vision for the concept of smart envirnoments. He presented a scenario in which the computer would become invisible and indistinguishable from its environment. In this scenario, interaction between humans and computers would not occur as usual -- where the humans devote their full atention to the computer --, but would consist of small and quick interactions, usually in different devices and locations, and in many times using the ``periphery'' of the user's attention \citep{weiser96}, or without the user even realizing he is interacting with a computer system. According to the author, ``the most profound technologies are those that disappear. They weave themselves into the fabric of everyday life until they are indistinguishable from it.'' \citep{weiser91}. He asserts that the future of computing lies in integrating the computer to the real world, not the other way around, and he calls this new paradigm Ubiquitous Computing.

However, the availability of computing resources everytime and everywhere poses the risk of information overload on the user. To solve this problem, the concept of ``calm technology'' was created \citep{weiser96}. This means the transfer of information between the computer and the user must happen in the ``periphery'' of the user's attention, moving to the ``center'' of his attention in moments of urgency. Taking a car's engine as an example: when a person drives a car, his attention is focused on the road, and he barely notices the engine sound. However, if the engine emits a strange sound (that might indicate some kind of problem), the driver's attention turns to the engine.

If a system is to be minimally intrusive, it must undestand the situation of the user and his surroundings, and adapt its own behaviour according to this information. This information is called context, and systems capable of this kind of adaptation are called context-sensitive \citep{satyanarayanan01,dey2001}. 

Research in this area shows that is not only useful to know the context of the user when a service is being offered, but that the history of visited contexts can be a valuable information to help the application to adapt itself to the user. This history of visited contexts is called Trail \citep{silva10}. Altough the trail is usually tied to the location of a user, the concept of a trail is breader and includes activities performed, applications used, content accessed -- among others -- inside a specific context and timeframe \citep{silva09}.

For an application to adapt itself effectively to a user, it is not only important to know the history of contexts visited by a user, but to understand who the user is -- that is, what are his characteristics. This kind of application organize its knowledge about a user in User Profiles. The profiles are stored in a format called User Model \citep{viviani10}.

Personalization means the individual adaptation of products, services and information. Personalization technology usually involve programs that learn patterns, habits and preferences of a user. The main goal of modern personalization systems is to offer the users what they want, without demanding them to inform it explicitly \citep{mulvenna00}. It is clear that this purpose meets the ubiquitous computing objective that is, the development of a technology that ``disappears''.

For a system to be succeessful in obtaining the user's intent, it must know the user, the context where the user is inserted and the actions he took when he was previously in the same context or in similar contexts. Although there are some studies that seek to infer knowledge about a user from their trail, previous works operate within a specific context (such as Ubiquitous Education \citep{barbosa11} or Ubiquitous Commerce \citep{franco11}) or use a static user model \citep{pelep}.

A relevant aspect for environments with multiple independent systems is the interoperability. Interoperability is the ability of two or more systems or components to exchange information and use the information that has been exchanged. \citep{ieeedict}. In the context of customization, interoperability refers to the ability to access and interpret information derived from multiple heterogeneous sources, and integrate it into a common user model. Interoperability can be (i) syntactic, where the differences between the models are resolved at application level, or (ii) semantic, where the differences between the models are resolved at the level of knowledge \citep{carmagnola09}.

Research indicates that it is desirable that future systems become increasingly invisible to their users \citep{weiser96}, and that a system can only become invisible if it is proactive, and can only be proactive by knowing the user \citep{satyanarayanan01}. For a system to know a user, he must know the user history, understand the context in which he is inserted, as well as data collected concerning him in the same context (or in a similar context), and be able to process this set of information into a user profile.

In this context we are proposing the eProfile, a model for the automatic creation of profiles by inference of entity trails. This model allows the use of a single profile by several independent systems, offering the possibility of semantic interoperability between them.

In this model, the concept of entity is used instead of the classic concept of User. While usually the ``user'' means the person who is using the application, an entity refers to any object (physical or not) able to control an application. Thus, an entity can be a person, a moving object (like a car) or property (like a room), or even a different system.

This article is organized as follows. Section~\ref{ch:modelo} proposes the eProfile model. Section~\ref{ch:implementacao} discusses implementation aspects. Section~\ref{ch:cenario} presents an experiment validating the model. Section~\ref{ch:relacionados} describes related work, showing the contributions of this model. Finally, a Section~\ref{sec:conclusao} presents the concluding remarks.

\section{eProfile Model}
\label{ch:modelo}

\begin{figure}
  \centering\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{imagens/modelobasico_en.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Overview of eProfile}}
  \label{fig:modelobasico}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:modelobasico} shows the overview of eProfile, where each circle represents a component, and the arrows indicate the flow of data.

The first component depicted is the {\bf application}. The application, in this model, is any application that is being used directly by an entity. For an application to be considered suitable to use this model and the functionality offered by eProfile, it needs to record their information on a trail manager supported by eProfile. The application must also implement a communication protocol to exchange messages with eProfile. If these prerequisites are followed, the application can be of any type and operate in any type of device that has communication with the Internet.

The second component depicted is a {\bf trail manager}. A trail manager is capable of storing events held by an entity, putting them in chronological order and registering information relevant to the event (eg, the context in which the event was held). The eProfile manager supports any trail manager whose communication protocol is implemented in eProfile.

The third component of Figure~\ref{fig:modelobasico} represents the {\bf eProfile}. The arrows indicate the flow of data. The communication starts when the application registers itself on eProfile, sending (i) the entity model used by the application and (ii) the rules for construction of this model (arrow ``a''). From this moment on, the application starts recording the events on the trail manager (arrow ``b''). The eProfile periodically retrieves this information from the trail manager and builds or reconfigures the profile entity (arrow ``c''). When the application needs an updated profile, it retrieves this profile from eProfile (arrow ``d'').

Since the eProfile supports multiple applications, it is possible for different applications to use different trail managers. Therefore, the eProfile also supports communication with a number of trail managers, as exemplified in Figure~\ref{fig:gerenciadores}.

  \caption{\textbf{eProfile connected to multiple trail managers}}

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{sec:arquitetura}

\begin{figure}
  \centering\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{imagens/modelo_big_en.png}
  \caption{\textbf{eProfile Architecture}}
  \label{fig:modelo}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:modelo} shows the architecture of eProfile. The components ``Application'' and ``Trail Manager'' are the same as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:modelobasico}, while the ``eProfile'' service was expanded to show its inner workings.

Since the eProfile is a model that infers information about data stored in an ontology autonomously, and is organized into independent parts, it uses the technology of autonomous agents.

Each application builds its own entity model. The eProfile needs three pieces of information to be able to process the profiles: (i) the entity trail, (ii) the entity model used by the application and (iii) the inference rules for the profile. This can be seen in Figure~\ref{fig:modelo}, where the agent ``Reasoner'' receives data from these three databases. The trail of the entity is loaded by the eProfile from the trail manager, while the other two information are sent directly from the application to the eProfile. Thus, the inference rules defined by the application are used to infer the profile of the entity.

The arrows in Figure~\ref{fig:modelo} indicate the direction of the data flow, where it can be noted two entry points and one exit point.

The first entry point (arrow ``a'') are the information sent from the application to eProfile or, more specifically, to the {\bf Configurator} agent. The following information must be sent by the application:

\begin{itemize}

\item the entity model used by the application, which is the format of the entity profile. Once the entity trail is processed, the inferred data is stored and made available for application using this format;

\item rules to infer the profile. These rules will be executed at regular intervals in order to update the entity profile. The Configurator receives the rules, validate them and insert them in \textbf{rule database}. Along with the rules, is specified the criticality of information. The system uses this information to determine how often the information will be inferred.

\end{itemize}

The second entry point (arrow ``c'') allows receiving the entity trails, which are fetched by the eProfile from the trail manager. Since eProfile can connect to different trail managers, (Figure~\ref{fig:gerenciadores}), and each trail manager might use one specific format, a conversion between the original format and the format used by eProfile is needed for the trails to be stored in a standardized manner. This work is performed by an agent named {\bf Converter}, which is able to perform the conversion to the eProfile internal trail format.

The entity trail is then stored in a trail ontology called {\bf eOntoTrail}. This ontology contains the basic classes supporting the construction of a trail, and they can be extended according to the specification of the trail manager that is being used.

Once in possession of the tracks, the model and rules, the \textbf{Reasoner} agent infers from the trail, generating a entity profile that consists of an extension of a basic profile ontology named {\bf eOntoProfile}. This ontology, just like the trail ontology, contains basic classes from which applications can specialize, using them to store their own information. The application can then connect to the eProfile and get its entity profile, whose data are sent by the \textbf{Communicator} agent (arrow ``d'').





\subsection{Ontologies}

As mentioned in Section~\ref{sec:arquitetura}, eProfile uses two ontologies: a trail ontology and a profile ontology. These are both basic ontologies, extended by subclasses according to the trail format sent by the trail manager (for the trail ontology) or the needs of the application (for the profile ontology).

The trail ontology data (eOntoTrail), shown in Figure~\ref{sec:ont_trilha}, is fed by different supported trail managers. The Converter agent is responsible for receiving data in the trail manager format and converting it to the eOntoTrail. This way, the reasoner has a single ontology on which to reason.

\begin{figure}
  \centering\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{imagens/ont_trilha_simples.png}
  \caption{\textbf{eProfile Trail Ontology}}
  \label{fig:ont_trilha}
\end{figure}

Context-aware applications are interested in knowing {\bf who}, {\bf where}, {\bf when} and {\bf what} (that is, what the user is doing), so they can discover why the situation is occurring \citep{abowd99}. Thus, a trail is represented by a set of events characterized by the following main classes: (i) {\em Entity}, the identity of the entity that is performing the action, which can optionally be linked to a profile, (ii) {\em Location}, the place where the action is taking place, (iii) {\em Time}, the date and time the event occurred, (iv) {\em Participants}, the entities linked to this action, and (v) {\em Activity}, the action that the entity is performing (or trying to perform). This class is the most comprehensive of the ontology, and will possibly be the most specialized by the trail managers.

\begin{figure}
  \centering\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{imagens/ont_perfil3.png}
  \caption{\textbf{eProfile Profile Ontology}}
  \label{fig:ont_perfil}
\end{figure}

The profile ontology (eOntoProfile), shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ont_perfil}, is the ontology used to store the entity models, as well as the profile information inferred from the trail. For the ontology to be feasible within the proposed model, it must be a generic ontology that allows its classes to be extended by applications. In the figure, a relationship between classes is shown by a dotted line, and a specialization of a class is shown by a continuous line. The eOntoProfile is a basic ontology, extended by the applications for them to assemble their own entity models.

The basic class of the ontology is the {\em Element} class. The class {\em Element} represents anything in the real world. It has the following children classes: (i) {\it Entity}, which represents an entity, that is, an element that has a profile associated with it, and (ii) {\it NonEntity}, which represents an element than doesn't have a profile associated with it. For example, a trail registers a event in which a person enters a store, but the store has no profile in registered in eProfile. In this case, the store is a {\it NonEntity}. This distinction is important because in the first case, the eProfile has access to a wide range of information about the entity, entities related to that first entity, and so on.

The class {\em Characteristic} represent a aspect of the entity, and is the class that will be extended by applications to refer to any information related to that entity. These characteristics can be simple labels (such as name of a person, or mileage of a car) or more complex information (such as academic knowledge or latest routes traveled).



\subsection{Rule Execution}

The execution of rules is key to eProfile's operation. A inference rule is a instruction issued by an application, that runs on a entity trailset, resulting in a entity profile according to a entity model previously registered by the application.

For a rule to be executed, the application must have previously registered within eProfile: (i) the rule itself, (ii) in an entity model, which serves as basis for the entity profile format, and (iii) the trail of the entity on which the inference is performed. From these three pieces of information, the reasoner is able to generate a profile entity, according to Figure~\ref{fig:dados_inferencia}.

\begin{figure}\centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{imagens/dados_inferencia_en.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Data flow for the entity profile inference}}
  \label{fig:dados_inferencia}
\end{figure}

The rules are always defined by the application, and a rule is composed of the following set of information: (i) name, (ii) profile field that will be updated, (iii) the rule, written in OWL language \citep{owl-guide}, (iv) update method (the value can be overwritten or updated according to a formula), (v) update frequency, and (vi) expiration of the rule.

\section{Implementation Aspects}
\label{ch:implementacao}

The eProfile prototype was developed in Python \citep{python}. Python is a generic, open source, object-oriented, high-level language, that supports multiple architectures. The databases are implemented using the SQLite3 database \citep{sqlite3}. SQLite3 is an SQL transactional self-contained database, available in the public domain. The SQLite3 was chosen due to its good performance and the fact that it does not require a separate server for deployment.

Figure~\ref{fig:dados_inferencia} shows the data flow for the entity profile inference process. At the heart of this operation is the Reasoner agent, which uses an external inference engine to implement the rules. The reasoner used is FACT++, a propositional logic reasoner created as a platform for use of algorithms of semantic {\em tableaux} and optimization techniques \citep{tsarkov06}. This reasoner was chosen due to its good performance and to support all functions used by the eProfile.

The knowledge representation language used for storage and processing of ontologies is OWL\cite {owl-guide}, chosen because for being the most expressive ontology language defined for the Semantic Web \citep{daconta03}.

\section{Evaluation Aspects}
\label{ch:cenario}

It is only possible to evaluate the functionalities of a proposed infrastructure by building applications that utilize it and then evaluate them, in order to obtain an indirect evaluation of the model \citep{edwards03}. The scientific community has been using scenarios to perform this type of indirect evaluation in context sensitive (such as the approach taken by \citet{dey2001b}) and ubiquitous (according to \citet{satyanarayanan01}) environments. This work follows the same approach -- we created a prototype and a scenario for performing an experiment, in order to evaluate eProfile.

The experiment presented is based on the generation of simulated trails of graduate students within the context of Ubiquitous Learning. Ubiquitous Learning can be understood as a set of educational processes that occur anywhere, at any time and with any device, continuously, contextualized and integrated into the daily lives of apprentices \cite{barbosa11}.

\begin{figure}
  \centering\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{imagens/modelo_cenario1.png}
  \caption{Scenario model}
  \label{fig:modelo_cenario1}
\end{figure}

The scenario is composed of four applications that interact with among themselves, according to Figure~\ref{fig:modelo_cenario1}. LOCAL is a ubiquitous learning system used to find learning opportunities and put students in touch. UniManager is an application used by the administration of the university to manage student grades and disciplines. Both softwares have profile modules, where the profile information is used for decision making. Since in both cases the profiles module is static and depends on collection of explicit information, the eProfile is connected to these softwares, leveraging the dynamic creation of profiles and collecting information implicitly. Furthermore, the eProfile enables interoperability between these systems by allowing information from both systems to be used to generate single profiles. The scenario also includes a trail managers, that manages the entities trails.

The initial communication occurs between applications and eProfile (arrows ``a'' and ``b''), where applications define their entity models and rules. This communication can occur as often as necessary, because applications can update their rules and their entity models continuously. During the execution of the applications, they record the events in the Trail Manager (arrows ``c'' and ``d''). This means applications can run disconnected from eProfile, and connect only when a rule needs to by updated or when profile data must be downloaded. eProfile retrieves trails periodically from the Trail Manager (arrow ``e''). When eProfile is in possession of the trails, rules and entity models, it starts to process these information, inferring new profile data. These profiles are then downloaded by applications (arrows ``f'' and ``g'').

The following sections describe the integration between eProfile, LOCAL (Section~\ref{sec:eprofile_local}) and UniManager (Section~\ref{sec:eprofile_unimanager}), the simulation environment (Section~\ref{sec:desc_cen1}), trails, rules and generated profiles (Section~\ref{sec:trilhas_cen1}), the simulation parameters (Section~\ref{sec:simulacao_cen1}) and results (Seção~\ref{sec:resultados_cen1}).

\subsection{eProfile/LOCAL Integration}
\label{sec:eprofile_local}

This scenario incorporates the eProfile to a ubiquitous learning system called LOCAL (Location and Context-Aware Learning). LOCAL uses location information and context as an aid to teaching and learning process. A tracking system monitors the mobility of apprentices and, based on their physical position, explores educational opportunities \cite{barbosa11}.

LOCAL, whose architecture is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:arq_local}, consists of seven components: (i) a system of user profiles, which stores data relevant to the process of teaching and learning, (ii) a system location, (iii) a Personal Assistant (PA), that follows the user on his mobile device, (iv) a repository of learning objects, (v) a system for sending contextualized messages, (vi) a task scheduling event system and (vii) the tutor, an analysis engine that performs inference using data provided by the profile and tranking systems.

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{imagens/arq_local.png}
  \caption{LOCAL architecture}
  \label{fig:arq_local}
\end{figure}

LOCAL's profile system information gathering is explicit. This means that users' (learners) relevant data need to be pre-populated in the system. Furthermore, the LOCAL profile system is static, which means that data is not automatically updated in response to actions taken by learners or external events. Since eProfile is a dynamic entity model with implicit information gathering, integrating these two models results in the enhancement of LOCAL capabilities.

LOCAL fits the eProfile model according to Figure~\ref{fig:eprofile_local}. Besides LOCAL and eProfile, \ref{fig:eprofile_local} presents an application called Trail Manager. A trail manager is an external program that stores the tracks, processes them and forwards them to the eProfile, as described in Section~\ref{sec:model}. For this scenario, we created a simple trail manager, which just receives, stores and forwards the trails to the eProfile upon request.

Integration between eProfile and LOCAL was based on the following changes to the LOCAL: (i) we included a connection interface with eProfile, able to generate learners entity models and rules for profile processing, (ii) we included a connection interface with the trail manager, for the tracking events and actions undertaken on the Personnel Assistant were to be sent to the trail manager, and (iii) we included a communication interface with the eProfile, where the LOCAL User Profiles Module is fed by profiles generated by eProfile.

The first step of integration occurs when the LOCAL communicates with eProfile for the first time. In that momento, LOCAL indicates to the eProfile what is the entity model, and the rules for generating the profiles. This set of information is registeren in eProfile trough the Configurator Agent (arrow `` a''), which organizes this information and registers it in the Models and Rules databases.

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{imagens/eprofile_local.png}
  \caption{eProfile/LOCAL Integration}
  \label{fig:eprofile_local}
\end{figure}

As learners use LOCAL, their location changes are identified by the Location System module, and their actions are done on the Personal Assistant. These modules record events in the Trail Manager (arrows ``b'' and ``c''), which in turn sends them to the Converter eProfile agent (arrow ``d''). Trails sent are stored in the trail database. These trails are periodically processed according to the models and rules defined initially, and a inference process based on this information generates profiles, which are sent to the LOCAL User Profiles module (arrow ``e'') by the communicator agent. These profiles help LOCAL know its users (learners) in order to distribute content in the most contextualized possible way.

\subsection{eProfile/UniManager Integration}
\label{sec:eprofile_unimanager}

The UniManager is a in administrative software used by the university for registration and management of students, faculty and disciplines. This application model is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:unimanager}.

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{imagens/unimanager.png}
  \caption{UniManager Model}
  \label{fig:unimanager}
\end{figure}

The UniManager has two interfaces: administrative and user. The administrative interface is used by the management of universities to enroll subjects, faculty members and students. It is also used by students to carry out their registration for the next semester. This interface connects to a disciplines manager, which manages the enrollment and prerequisites between disciplines.

The user interface is used in the professors' and students' mobile devices, and is connected to the university tracking system. Thus, all events recorded in UniManager from this interface contains the location of the student within the University, that is, the room where the student is. The tracking system integrated to the user interface allows the system to check whether the student or the professor are present in class. The student performs the tests and hands out papers using this interface, and the professor evaluates the student tests and papers.

UniManager also contains a profile database. On this database are stored: (i) registration information of the disciplines, (ii) presences and absences of professors and students, and (iii) tests and papers hand out by the students, and students evaluations about the professor.

The profile database is dynamically updated based on recorded events, but is limited to data recorded in UniManager itself. Integration between eProfile and UniManager leverages the generation of more advanced profiles, using the information inferred by eProfile from the data generated by LOCAL. Using this data, it is possible to register the interests of the student in UniManager's profile database, in order to suggest him optional subjects that might be of interest. Furthermore, the use of trails allows the profiles to be generated based on historical information, obtaining profile information that can be useful in administrative analysis (eg, what combination of professor and discipline historically resulted in higher grades for students in each discipline).

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{imagens/unimanager_eprofile.png}
  \caption{UniManager/eProfile Integration}
  \label{fig:unimanager_eprofile}
\end{figure}

The UniManager integrates to eProfile according to Figure~\ref{fig:unimanager_eprofile}. In the first communication between UniManager and eProfile,  UniManager sends a set of messages indicating to eProfile which are the entity models and the rules used to generate the profiles (arrow ``a''). The registration of this information is done in the configurator agent, responsible for validating and storing the rules and models in their respective databases. As users make use of the UniManager interfaces, data is processed and stored by the Disciplines, Faculty and Student Manager modules, which store the events in the trail manager, along with its location (arrows ``b'', ``c'' and ``d''). These trails are organized and stored in the trail manager, and it periodically sends the trails to the converter agent in eProfile (arrow ``e''). The converter agent converts the trails to the eProfile format and stores them in the trail database. Once in possession of the trails, models and rules, the reasoner agent processes the information and generates profiles that are sent to UniManager by the communicator agent (arrow ``f'').

\subsection{Environment}
\label{sec:desc_cen1}

The scenario consists of a group of students that carries out a graduation course in Computer Science. The course consists of a total of 40 disciplines, among which 12 are optional (the student must complete at least 8 out of these 12). All disciplines have other disciplines as prerequisites, except for the disciplines of the beginning of the course (Figure~\ref{fig:disciplinas} exemplifies a subset of subjects with their prerequisites). Each discipline has a professor, a limited number of openings for students, and a set of resources (such as special rooms or equipment).

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{imagens/disciplinas.png}
  \caption{Prerequisites of some disciplines}
  \label{fig:disciplinas}
\end{figure}

Os alunos e docentes utilizam dispositivos móveis ({\em laptop} ou {\em smartphones}), onde estão instalados aplicativos utilizados para a administração de sua vida acadêmica. Em especial, estão instalados o UniManager e o LOCAL.

O UniManager é o sistema utilizado pela universidade para administração das disciplinas, descrito na Seção~\ref{sec:eprofile_unimanager}. Ele é usado pela administração da universidade, pelos professores e estudantes. A administração utiliza-o para cadastrar disciplinas, alunos e docentes,  vincular os alunos e docentes às disciplinas e emitir relatórios gerenciais, como lista de presenças e aprovação/reprovação de alunos, bem como faltas e avaliações de professores. Os docentes utilizam o sistema para realizar as avaliações dos alunos. Os estudantes o utilizam para realizar suas provas e trabalhos, para fazer sua matrícula nas disciplinas e para verificar suas notas e faltas.

Além disso, os docentes e os alunos também utilizam o LOCAL em seus dispositivos móveis. O LOCAL combina as informações dos perfis com sua localização, de modo a poder assisti-los, facilitando a interação entre os aprendizes e permitindo a entrega de material pedagógico contextualizado. Os docentes utilizam o LOCAL para cadastrar o material pedagógico que será distribuído aos alunos. O LOCAL se comunica com os estudantes de modo a distribuir o material pedagógico previamente cadastrado e colocar estudantes com interesses similares em contato durante a realização de atividades pedagógicas. Caso, durante estas atividades, o estudante não tenha nenhum interesse cadastrado que corresponda àqueles requeridos pela atividade, ele tem a opção de fazer a escolha utilizando o Assistente Pessoal.

\subsection{Trilhas, Regras e Perfis}
\label{sec:trilhas_cen1}

Segundo descrito nas Seções~\ref{sec:eprofile_local} e \ref{sec:eprofile_unimanager}, os aplicativos LOCAL e UniManager sofreram três alterações significativas: (i) foram incluídas {\em interfaces} de comunicação com o eProfile, para que os aplicativos fornecessem os modelos de entidade e as regras, (ii) foram incluídas {\em interfaces} de comunicação com o Gerenciador de Trilhas, de modo que os aplicativos pudessem informar os eventos ocorridos e (iii) o módulo de perfis de ambas as aplicações foi modificado de modo a solicitar as atualizações de perfil a partir do eProfile.

O cenário é composto por três entidades: (i) estudantes, (ii) docentes e (iii) disciplinas. As entidades estudante e docente representam, respectivamente, cada um dos alunos e professores envolvidos no curso. A entidade disciplina, que refere-se a cada uma das disciplinas do curso, poderia ser representada tanto como uma entidade quanto como um simples recurso. Neste cenário, foi decidido que ela seria representada como uma entidade, pois desta forma é possível permitir que ela crie uma trilha, da qual pode ser obtido um perfil. Desta forma, o perfil da disciplina pode conter campos que são atualizados repetidamente, tal como a nota média da disciplina.

Segundo descrito na Seção~\ref{sec:eontotrail}, aplicações sensíveis a contexto estão interessadas em saber {\bf quem}, {\bf onde}, {\bf quando} e {\bf o quê} (isto é, o que o usuário está fazendo), de forma a poderem descobrir por que a situação está ocorrendo. A ontologia de trilha do eProfile, mostrada na Figura~\ref{fig:ont_trilha}, define que estas questões são respondidas, respectivamente, pelas classes {\em Identity}, {\em Location}, {\em Time} e {\em Activity}. Além disso, a classe {\em Participants} descreve as outras entidades envolvidas neste evento. Quando uma trilha é armazenada no eProfile, a informação referente ao evento deve ser herdada destas classes, utilizando tantas classes quantas forem relevantes para que a informação esteja completa.

A trilha dos estudantes contém os seguintes eventos: cadastro, matrícula em disciplina, entrada e saída em aula, realização de provas e trabalhos, escolha de preferências e abandono de curso. O formato da trilha está detalhado na Figura~\ref{fig:ont_estudante}. Na trilha dos docentes são registrados os seguintes eventos: cadastro, entrada e saída em aula e avaliação das provas e trabalhos dos alunos, segundo detalhando na Figura~\ref{fig:ont_docente}. A trilha da disciplina recebe os seguintes eventos: cadastro e início e fim de semestre, de acordo com a Figura~\ref{fig:ont_disciplina}.

O objetivo final da geração das trilhas é a geração dos perfis. Para que o eProfile tenha sucesso em gerar os perfis a partir das trilhas, ele necessita que o aplicativo informe a ele os modelos de entidades e as regras de inferência.

Os modelos de entidades são a estrutura de dados utilizada para armazenar o perfil de uma entidade. O modelo de entidade utilizado pelo módulo de Perfis de Usuários do LOCAL é mostrado na Figura~\ref{fig:perfil_local}. Os perfis dos aprendizes apresentam dois tipos de dados: (i) persistentes, relacionados ao aprendiz em todas as situações que ele se encontre e (ii) contextuais, associado com os contextos com as quais o aprendiz interage.

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{imagens/perfil_local.png}
  \caption{Modelo de perfis do LOCAL}
  \label{fig:perfil_local}
\end{figure}

As seções do bloco persistente do perfil são (i) contato, que armazena dados básicos do usuário, tais como nome, telefone, entre outros, (ii) preferências, que guarda o tipo de mídia preferida pelo usuário e (iii) interesses, que identifica tópicos de interesse dos alunos. A seção de interesses está categorizada de acordo com o sistema de classificação computacional da ACM \cite{acm10}. As seções do bloco contextual são (i) relações, que identifica os relacionamentos com outros usuários dentro do mesmo contexto, (ii) performance, que diz respeito a objetivos que devem ser atingidos pelos estudantes e (iii) segurança, que armazena credenciais necessárias para acessar diferentes níveis em contextos específicos.

O UniManager armazena perfis de disciplinas, docentes e estudantes, e tem seu modelo de entidade ilustrado na Figura~\ref{fig:perfil_unimanager}. As informações básicas, disciplinas matriculadas e notas/faltas do perfil do estudante são utilizadas para atividades gerenciais da Universidade, como verificar se o aluno pode cursar uma determinada disciplina, assim como as informações de disciplinas do docente. As informações de desempenho do docente e de médias históricas das disciplinas são utilizadas para ajustar o docente com a disciplina que tenha maior afinidade ou desempenho. As informações de interesse dos alunos são utilizadas para sugerir disciplinas opcionais aos alunos.

O UniManager e o LOCAL definem um conjunto de regras para geração dos perfis baseados nas trilhas geradas por ambos os aplicativos. Um aplicativo tem a possibilidade de utilizar a trilha de outro aplicativo para o processamento das regras e consequente geração dos perfis, permitido assim que o perfil da entidade seja enriquecido pelo ambiente que o cerca.

A definição das regras é feita em OWL, através da criação de classes que possam ser processadas por um raciocinador. A Tabela~\ref{tab:aula} mostra o exemplo de uma aula onde o professor cadastra no LOCAL um conjunto de assuntos que serão debatidos em aula (evento 1). Este evento gera uma trilha que é enviada do LOCAL ao Gerenciador de Trilhas e, subsequentemente, ao eProfile.

O LOCAL, então, tenta identificar os alunos que possuam iteresses iguais aos do debate proposto, ou interesses que estejam relacionadas a estes (evento 7). Desta forma, o LOCAL envia as regras OWL descritas na Figura~\ref{fig:regra1} para realizar esta identificação. A primeira regra chama-se {\em RelatedSubjects} (linha 2), onde são inferidos todos os assuntos (classe {\em Subject}, linha 4) que sejam relacionados (linha 6) aos assuntos que serão debatidos em aula (linha 7). A expressão {\tt ?subject} é uma extensão do eProfile, que substitui esta expressão pelos assuntos que estão sendo utilizados, permitindo que a mesma regra possa ser usada em diferentes situações. Esta regra, após inferida, resulta na listagem de todos os assuntos (classes do tipo {\em Subject}) que se relacionam aos assuntos que serão debatidos, colocando estas classes como subclasses da classe {\em RelatedSubjects}.

A partir desta regra, é possível buscar os estudantes (linha 15) cujos interesses se intersectam com os interesses encontrados na classe {\em RelatedSubjects} (linha 18). Estes estudantes, na ontologia, passam a ser subclasses da classe {\em StudentsSelected} (linha 13).

\subsection{Protótipo e Simulação}
\label{sec:simulacao_cen1}

O protótipo do eProfile foi desenvolvido na linguagem Python \cite{python}. Ele utiliza o HermiT como motor de inferência \cite{motik09}. A comunicação entre estes dois aplicativos se dá via troca de arquivos: quando é necessário que seja feito o processamento de algum perfil, o eProfile gera um arquivo OWL contendo as regras de processamento e chama o HermiT, que processa a ontologia e salva um novo arquivo com a ontologia gerada. O eProfile lê então este arquivo para dar prosseguimento ao processo.

Para a simulação do modelo, foram desenvolvidos dois programas:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Um simulador, que une as funcionalidades do UniManager e do LOCAL gerando trilhas simuladas dos estudantes, dos docentes e das disciplinas;
  \item Um gerenciador de trilhas simulado, que armazena as trilhas geradas e disponibiliza-as para o eProfile.
\end{enumerate}

A comunicação entre estes três aplicativos (os dois aplicativos simulados e o eProfile) é feita através de {\em webservices} utilizando REST \cite{fieldings00}.

Para simular o cenário de forma mais fiel possível, cada uma das entidades é criada com um conjunto de características que as distinguem umas das outras, e faz com que tenham desempenhos diferentes e tomem decisões diferentes quando confrontadas com uma escolha. Os docentes são gerados com valores aleatórios de experiência e rigor. Estas características influem diretamente no nível de aprendizado dos estudantes, na dificuldade da disciplina e na probabilidade de desistência dos alunos. Os estudantes são gerados com níveis variados de empenho, inteligência e gostos pessoais. Estas características influenciam o desempenho do aluno, a probabilidade de desistência das disciplinas e do curso, e as escolhas que o aluno irá realizar.

O cenário simula um conjunto de 40 alunos que realiza um curso de ciência da computação em uma Universidade. O tempo da simulação é aberto, sendo que a mesma será executada até que todos os alunos se formem ou tenham desistido do curso.

Uma simulação inicia com a criação das entidades de disciplinas, estudantes e docentes. São então gerados os perfis iniciais das disciplinas, e os docentes são distribuídos aleatoriamente entre as disciplinas. A partir daí, inicia-se um {\em loop} que termina apenas quando todos os alunos tiverem concluído ou abandonado o curso. Cada iteração do {\em loop} representa um semestre do curso contém os seguintes passos:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Os alunos escolhem as disciplinas que irão cursar e se matriculam;
  \item Cada dia da disciplina é simulado, onde os alunos assistem a aula e realizam as provas ou entregam os trabalhos;
  \item A nota final dos alunos é calculada, e os alunos decidem se continuarão ou abandonarão o curso.
\end{enumerate}

Cada disciplina é composta por 14 aulas. Existem dois tipos de eventos que podem ocorrer em uma aula: (i) um trabalho ou debate em grupo, e (ii) um trabalho ou prova individual, sendo que uma aula pode ter um dos dois eventos, ambos ou nenhum. A Tabela~\ref{tab:aula} exemplifica uma aula em que os dois tipos de eventos ocorreram.
